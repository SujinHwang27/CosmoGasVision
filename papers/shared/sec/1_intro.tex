\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

This paper, in computer-vision language, is a sparse-view tomography problem with an unusual sensing modality. The volume to be reconstructed is the \textbf{intergalactic medium} (IGM) --- the diffuse gas between galaxies, which holds most of the universe's baryonic matter and forms the filaments and voids of the cosmic web. No camera images this volume directly. We observe it through \textbf{Lyman-alpha forest} spectra: light from a distant quasar crosses the IGM, neutral hydrogen along the path absorbs photons at a resonance wavelength, and cosmic-expansion Doppler shifts smear the absorption into a dense forest of narrow dips. Each 1D spectrum is a \textbf{sightline} --- one ray through the volume, the analogue of one CT scanline --- and the position of every dip encodes the gas state along that ray.

Reconstructing the 3D IGM from a sparse bundle of such 1D sightlines is therefore tomography from pencil-beam projections, known as \emph{Lyman-alpha tomography}~\cite{stark2015tomography} in astrophysics and as sparse-view inverse rendering with a known forward operator in CV vocabulary. Classical solutions TARDIS~\cite{horowitz2019tardis} and Wiener filtering~\cite{stark2015tomography} discretize the volume into a fixed voxel grid. At typical inter-sightline spacing (several $h^{-1}$ Mpc transverse), this either over-commits memory or aliases the small-scale absorption structure the metrics care about.

\textbf{Motivation.} Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)~\cite{mildenhall2020nerf} replaced voxel grids with a coordinate-conditioned MLP, supervised through a differentiable rendering integral. The IGM problem needs the same move. We are not rendering color; we are recovering a physical state from absorption profiles, and we want gradients that flow through the forward physics rather than through a discretization seam.

\textbf{Contribution.} We introduce \emph{CosmoGasVision}, a NeRF-style continuous field for cosmological gas coupled to a differentiable projector that maps the MLP-decoded fields to 1D Lyman-alpha optical depth (a log measure of light absorbed along the sightline; see \cref{sec:method}) via the analytic Voigt-Hjerting kernel~\cite{tepper2006voigt}. The contribution is the representation-plus-forward-model package: a continuous IGM field optimized end-to-end against the physics astrophysicists already trust, with sub-voxel recovered structures and a physically interpretable gradient path.

\textbf{What this paper is, and is not.} This paper does not demonstrate closed-loop 3D IGM tomography from sparse Ly$\alpha$ sightlines. At the corrected anchor and full publication schedule, two of three primary structure gates close at single-seed (mean-flux $4/4$ at seed=42, bootstrap $2/4$ at the population CI; KS $3/4$). The binding inertial-range $P_F$ residual fails $4/4$ at $\PFfactorOverGateLo\times$--$\PFfactorOverGateHi\times$ the gate. A $\tau$-binned residual decomposition positively localizes the saturation band $F \in [\SatBandLo, \SatBandHi]$ as the residually-supported mechanism cross-physics. Four counterfactual interventions tested under a pre-registered falsification discipline retire under previously-unaudited degeneracy classes. The remaining gap is residually supported but not closed.

\textbf{What this paper is.} (i) A continuous-neural-field formulation of Ly$\alpha$ tomography with a differentiable RSD-convolved Voigt integrator, a saturated-absorber mask, and a mean-flux soft anchor at the verified low-$z$ value~\cite{kirkman2007}; (ii) a cost-survey-validated training schedule with a closed-form linear-VRAM model across feedback physics and sightline-density tiers; (iii) a calibration-vs-structure falsification surfacing $P_F$ --- not anchor calibration, not sightline density alone --- as the binding gate, with the deficit residually localized to the saturation band cross-physics; (iv) a falsification-disciplined methodology of pre-committed structure gates, degeneracy-aware counterfactuals, and pre-committed stop conditions, so negative results become explicit constraints on the successor design.

The canonical classical IGM-tomography baselines are TARDIS~\cite{horowitz2019tardis} (Bayesian Wiener-filter reconstruction) and Stark+ Wiener filtering~\cite{stark2015tomography}. We position the continuous-neural-field formulation against these as qualitative comparison; numerical reproduction of either is out of scope.
